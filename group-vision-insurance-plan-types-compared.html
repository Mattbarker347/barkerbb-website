Coverage, Compared

Group vision insurance plan types compared.

Vision plans look simple, and mostly they are, but a few real differences decide which one fits your team and your budget. Here's what actually varies and how to read it.

Where group vision plans actually differ

Most group vision plans cover the same basics, a yearly exam and an allowance toward glasses or contacts. The differences come down to how the plan is funded, how generous the allowances are, the network, and whether it stands alone or bundles with other coverage.

Because the core of vision coverage is consistent, the comparison is less about wildly different products and more about the dials you can turn. Here are the ones that matter.

Funding: employer-paid or voluntary

The biggest fork. An employer-paid plan is a clean perk that costs the company a little per employee. A voluntary plan lets employees pay through payroll at group rates, so the company can offer it at no direct cost. Vision is one of the most common voluntary lines because the price is low either way.

Allowances and frequency

Plans differ on how much they put toward frames, lenses, and contacts, and how often, often every twelve or twenty-four months. A richer allowance and a yearly frequency cost a bit more but get used more. We match the level to what your team actually wants.

Network and standalone vs bundled

Networks vary, so we check that your team's providers are covered. Vision can stand alone as its own plan or bundle alongside dental or medical, which can simplify administration. Which is cleaner depends on your other coverage.

How to choose

Decide how you want to fund it, how generous you want the allowance, and whether bundling simplifies things on your end. From there it's a quick build. We quote the options side by side and right-size it so vision is an easy win rather than another decision to agonize over.
