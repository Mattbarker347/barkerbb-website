Benefits Questions, Answered

What's the minimum participation rate for group health insurance?

Carriers don't just want you to offer a plan, they want enough of your team to actually enroll. That threshold is called the participation rate, and it trips up more small groups than almost anything else. Here's how it works.

Why carriers care how many people enroll

A participation rate is the share of your eligible employees who have to sign up for the group plan before a carrier will issue it. The rule exists so carriers aren't only covering the people who expect to use it the most, which keeps the group's pricing stable.

The number isn't the same everywhere, but the idea is consistent across carriers. Here's what usually goes into it.

The typical threshold

Many carriers look for around 70 percent of eligible employees to enroll, though the exact figure varies by carrier and plan. It's a common reason a plan gets held up, so it's worth checking before you count on a specific start date.

Who counts, and who doesn't

This is the part that saves a lot of groups. Employees who already have other qualifying coverage, like a spouse's plan or Medicare, can usually be set aside, called a valid waiver, so they don't count against your participation math. Once you remove valid waivers, the percentage is often easier to hit than it first looks.

What happens if you fall short

If you can't meet the threshold, you still have paths. Some carriers ease participation rules during a special window late in the year, and other plan structures have different requirements. We work the waivers, check the carrier rules, and find the route that gets your plan issued.

That's a big part of what we do. We run the participation math before you're committed to anything, so there are no surprises when the plan goes to the carrier.
