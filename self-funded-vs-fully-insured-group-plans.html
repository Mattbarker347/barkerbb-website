Funding, Compared

Self-funded vs fully insured group plans.

The choice here is about who carries the risk for your employees' claims. One hands it entirely to the carrier; the other keeps it with you, with protection built in. Here's how each works and who it tends to fit.

The core difference is who pays the claims

With a fully insured plan you pay a fixed premium and the carrier covers whatever the claims turn out to be. With a self-funded plan you pay your employees' claims directly, usually with stop-loss insurance to cap the risk, and you keep the savings if the year runs healthy.

This fork used to be a big-company decision, but it reaches smaller Minnesota employers now, partly because of level funding, which we'll get to. Neither structure is better on its own. It depends on your group's health and your appetite for some variability.

How a fully insured plan works

You pay a set premium and the carrier takes on all the claims risk. Your cost for the year is locked, no matter what your team's claims look like. It's simple and predictable, which is why it's the default for most small groups. The trade-off is that if your group runs healthy, the savings stay with the carrier, not you.

How a self-funded plan works

You pay claims as they come in, and you carry stop-loss insurance so a bad year can't blow past a set ceiling. If your group is healthy, you keep what you don't spend, and you get real insight into where the money goes. The trade-off is more variability month to month, which is why it has traditionally fit larger, healthier groups.

Where level funding fits

Level funding is the middle path that brings self-funding within reach of a smaller group. You pay a steady monthly amount that covers expected claims, stop-loss, and administration, with a shot at a refund if claims run low and a cap if they run high. For a healthy small group, it's often the sweet spot.
