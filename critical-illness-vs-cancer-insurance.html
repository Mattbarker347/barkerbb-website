Coverage, Compared

Critical illness vs cancer insurance.

Both of these pay your employee a lump sum of cash when a serious diagnosis lands, which makes them easy to confuse. The difference is range versus depth, and it changes which one fits your team.

Broad coverage, or deep coverage on one disease

Critical illness insurance pays a lump sum across a list of serious conditions, like heart attack, stroke, and cancer. Cancer insurance pays specifically and more deeply when cancer is diagnosed and treated. One spreads the protection wide, the other concentrates it.

These are both voluntary supplemental plans that pay cash directly to the employee, on top of their medical coverage. The question is where you want that protection aimed.

How critical illness insurance works

It pays a lump-sum benefit when an employee is diagnosed with one of several covered conditions, commonly heart attack, stroke, and cancer among others. The strength is breadth, one plan that responds to a range of major health events. For most teams, that wide coverage is the practical choice.

How cancer insurance works

It focuses on a single disease, paying benefits tied to the diagnosis and the stages of cancer treatment. The strength is depth, more targeted support through what can be a long and expensive process. It tends to appeal where cancer is a particular worry, sometimes layered alongside a critical illness plan rather than instead of it.

How to choose between them

Ask whether you want protection that spreads across several major conditions, or extra depth on the one disease many people fear most. Critical illness is the broader, more common pick. Cancer coverage is worth considering when your team wants that focused support, and the two can work together. Either way, both are usually voluntary, so the company can offer them at little or no direct cost.
