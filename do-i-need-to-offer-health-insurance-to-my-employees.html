Benefits Questions, Answered

Do I have to offer health insurance to my employees?

It's one of the first questions Minnesota business owners ask us, and the honest answer is: it depends on your size. Here's how it actually works, without the legal jargon.

The short answer comes down to your headcount

Under federal law, whether you're required to offer health insurance depends on how many full-time employees you have. Most small businesses are not required to, but plenty choose to anyway, and there's a good reason for that.

The rule that matters here is the Affordable Care Act's employer shared responsibility provision. It splits employers into two groups based on size, and which group you're in changes everything.

If you have fewer than 50 full-time employees

You're not required to offer health insurance at all. The large majority of Minnesota small businesses fall here. Even so, plenty of owners in this group still offer coverage, because it's one of the biggest levers you have for hiring and keeping good people, and benefits are part of how a small team competes with a bigger one.

If you have 50 or more full-time employees

You're considered an applicable large employer, and you're generally expected to offer coverage that meets affordability and minimum-value standards to your full-time staff, or you can face a penalty. The math around full-time-equivalent counts gets technical fast, and that's a spot where it pays to have someone walk through it with you.

If you want to offer coverage either way

You've got more options than a traditional group plan. Depending on your team, an ICHRA can let you reimburse employees for individual coverage tax-free, and cooperative models like Health Access Solutions open up another path. We sit down, look at your size and budget, and lay out the routes that actually fit instead of pushing one product.
