Plan Types, Compared

PPO vs HMO for your small business.

Two of the most common plan types, and the difference comes down to flexibility versus cost. Here's how each one works so you can pick what's aligned with how your team actually uses care.

The real difference is networks and referrals

A PPO trades a higher price for more freedom, and an HMO trades some flexibility for a lower price and simpler structure. Neither is better in the abstract. The right one depends on your people and your budget.

When we model plans for a Minnesota small business, this is one of the first forks in the road. Both can be a great fit. It just depends on how your team likes to get care and what you're trying to spend.

How a PPO works

A PPO, or preferred provider organization, gives your employees a wide network and the freedom to see specialists without a referral. They can also go out of network and still get some coverage, just at a higher cost. That flexibility usually comes with a higher premium, so it tends to fit teams that value choice or have employees who travel or want to keep a specific doctor.

How an HMO works

An HMO, or health maintenance organization, asks each employee to pick a primary care physician and get referrals to see specialists. Care stays inside the network except in emergencies. In exchange, premiums and out-of-pocket costs are often lower, and the structure is simpler. It tends to fit teams that are cost-focused and mostly use in-network local care.

How to choose between them

Think about three things: what your team values, where they get care, and what the budget allows. If your people want maximum choice, a PPO leans your way. If you're trying to keep costs down and your team is happy staying local and in-network, an HMO is worth a hard look. A lot of employers also land somewhere in between with an HSA-compatible plan.
