Options, Compared

Voluntary vs employer-paid benefits.

Almost every benefits decision runs into this fork: does the company pay for it, or does the employee? The answer shapes your cost, your recruiting pitch, and what your team actually signs up for. Here's how to think about it.

The difference is who pays the premium

Employer-paid benefits are funded by the company, fully or in part. Voluntary benefits are paid by the employee through payroll, at group rates. Most real benefits packages use a mix, and getting the mix right is most of the art.

This isn't really a versus. The strongest packages blend the two. But it helps to be clear on what each one does for you and your team.

How employer-paid benefits work

The company covers the premium, fully or with a set contribution. These carry the most weight in recruiting and retention, because they're a clear, visible investment in your team. Medical and a basic group life policy are the common employer-paid foundation. The trade-off is direct cost to the business, which is why the contribution level is a real decision.

How voluntary benefits work

Employees choose and pay for these through payroll, at group rates they couldn't get on their own. Accident, critical illness, hospital indemnity, and extra life coverage are typical. The strength is that you can offer a richer, broader package at little or no direct cost to the company, while still giving your team real value and group pricing.

How to build the right mix

The usual approach is an employer-paid foundation, medical and basic life, with a layer of voluntary options on top so employees can tailor their own protection. That keeps the company's cost predictable while making the overall package feel deep. The right balance depends on your budget and what you're competing against for talent.

We help you set the foundation at the right level and choose the voluntary lines that round it out, so the package works for your budget and your people.
