HRA, HSA, FSA: what's the difference?

Three acronyms, three sets of rules, and a lot of confusion. They all help with healthcare costs, but who owns the money and what happens to it at year-end are completely different. Here's the plain-English version.

Same goal, very different rules

An HRA, an HSA, and an FSA all give employees a tax-advantaged way to handle medical costs. The differences come down to who funds the account, who owns it, and whether the money rolls over. Getting that right changes which one fits your group.

Here's each one in plain terms, and where it tends to fit.

HSA: Health Savings Account

The employee owns it, and it's paired with a qualifying high deductible health plan. The money rolls over year to year, it's portable when someone leaves, and both the employer and the employee can contribute up to the annual IRS limit. Think of it as a long-term, employee-owned account.

FSA: Flexible Spending Account

The employer sets it up, the employee elects an amount for the year, and contributions are pre-tax. The catch is the use-it-or-lose-it rule, with only limited carryover, so it works for costs an employee can reasonably predict. It isn't portable when someone leaves.

HRA: Health Reimbursement Arrangement

This one is funded and owned by the employer. The company sets an amount and reimburses employees for eligible costs. There are several flavors, including an ICHRA, which lets you reimburse employees tax-free for individual coverage instead of running a group plan. The rules are flexible, which is the point.

Which one fits depends on your plan and your goals. We walk through them with you and set up whichever actually serves your team, instead of pushing one acronym.
