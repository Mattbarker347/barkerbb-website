Coverage, Compared

Group term life vs whole life for employers.

When an employer offers life insurance, this is the question underneath it. The two products are built for different jobs, and knowing which is which keeps your benefits dollars working where they should. Here's the plain version.

Coverage for a season, or coverage for life

Group term life covers an employee for a set period, usually while they work for you, at a low cost and with no cash value. Whole life is permanent coverage that lasts a lifetime and builds cash value, at a much higher cost. For an employer benefit, those differences matter a lot.

Most employers who offer life insurance are really asking how to give their team meaningful protection without overspending. That framing makes the comparison clearer.

How group term life works

Term life covers a set period, and in a group plan that period is generally tied to employment. There's no cash value, which is exactly why it's affordable, and a basic employer-paid policy is often set at one or two times salary. It's the standard building block of a benefits package because it delivers real protection for a small cost, and employees can frequently add voluntary coverage for themselves and their families.

How whole life works

Whole life is permanent. It stays in force for a lifetime as long as it's funded, and part of the premium builds cash value the policyholder can borrow against. That permanence and savings feature come at a much higher cost, which is why whole life is usually an individual purchase rather than a core group benefit. Some employers offer it as a voluntary, employee-paid option alongside the group plan.

How employers usually handle it

The common setup is straightforward: employer-paid group term life as the foundation, with voluntary buy-up so employees who want more, or who want permanent coverage, can add it on their own. That keeps the company's cost predictable while giving the team room to tailor their own protection.
