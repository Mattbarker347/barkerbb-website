Who You Work With, Compared

Broker vs general agency for group benefits.

These two terms get mixed up a lot, and the difference matters when you're deciding who actually handles your company's benefits. One is the advisor on your side of the table. The other is back-office plumbing most employers never deal with directly. Here's how they fit together.

A broker works for you, a general agency works for brokers

A broker is the licensed advisor an employer hires to shop carriers, set up the plans, run enrollment, and stay the point of contact all year. A general agency sits one step back, between brokers and the big carriers, providing quoting technology, underwriting support, and carrier access. As an employer, the person you actually want on your side is the broker.

Almost every business that offers group benefits works with a broker, even if they've never heard the words "general agency." That's by design. The general agency layer is meant to stay invisible to you.

So when you're choosing who to trust with your benefits, the real question isn't broker or general agency. It's whether the person advising you is genuinely accountable to you, and whether they have the carrier access and tools to actually shop the market on your behalf.

What a broker does

A broker is licensed to represent your company. They learn your team and your budget, shop the Minnesota carriers, lay the options side by side, handle the enrollment paperwork, and pick up the phone when a claim or a billing question goes sideways. They stay your Broker of Record, which simply means the carrier recognizes them as your official representative. A good broker sits on the same side of the table as you all year, not just at renewal.

What a general agency does

A general agency, sometimes called a GA or a wholesale agency, is an intermediary between brokers and insurance carriers. Employers generally don't work with one directly. The big carriers don't hand out direct appointments to every small agency, so a general agency pools the volume of many brokers, which opens the door to carrier access, multi-carrier quoting platforms, and underwriting help that a small shop couldn't get on its own. Think of it as shared infrastructure sitting behind the broker.

How they actually work together

In practice you don't pick one over the other. You hire a broker, and a capable broker quietly taps general-agency infrastructure when it helps you, so you get fast quotes across more carriers and stronger options, while you still have one accountable person to call. The general agency takes its piece from the carrier side, so it doesn't change what you pay.

The thing to watch for is the opposite case, where there's no real advisor and you're just a census being run through a quoting tool. That's where the service quietly disappears.
